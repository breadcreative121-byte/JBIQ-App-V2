02 · Pattern map

Three archetypes the market has converged on.

Every app above sits somewhere on a spectrum from takeover (voice owns the screen) to ambient (voice runs in the periphery). For an app retro-fitting voice, the question isn't "what should our orb look like" — it's "where on this spectrum do we want to live."

Takeover

Centred orb / blob

Big, animated, fullscreen. Voice is treated as a destination. Best for dedicated assistants where the conversation is the product.

Examples: ChatGPT, Pi, Gemini Live, Google Assistant.

Hybrid

Bottom sheet / chip

Voice gets its own UI region but sits on top of the existing screen. The user keeps their bearings. Best for apps where voice is one input among many.

Examples: Meta AI banner, Alexa light bar, Perplexity chip.

Ambient

Edge glow / inline

Voice barely visible — a halo, a coloured outline, a tiny pulsing dot. Designed to disappear so the underlying app keeps working.

Examples: Siri iOS 18, Otter waveform, Humane Pin.

Cross-cutting moves

Live transcription is now non-negotiable. Even ambient designs show the user what the system "heard." It's the single biggest lever on user trust.

Breath beats spin. A 2–4s breath cycle reads as alive; a hard spinner reads as machine. ChatGPT, Pi and Siri all breathe; Grok span — and feels colder for it.

Interruptibility is a visual contract. If the orb only swells, users assume turn-based. If it always shows a "tap to interrupt" affordance, they speak over it.

For a retro-fit specifically

Don't build a fullscreen orb. The market is moving the other way — even OpenAI dropped the takeover. A new takeover screen will feel dated by the time it ships.

Pick a silhouette that already exists in your design system. If your brand has rounded chips, use a chip. If it has a strong colour, use a glow in that colour. Voice should look like your app, not like ChatGPT.

Design the four states as one family. The biggest mistake is designing "the listening screen" — design the transitions and the family resemblance instead.

03 · Wardrobe

Six directions for your product.

Each direction is shown in all four states (idle · listening · thinking · speaking). They run from quietest to loudest. Pick the silhouette that sits most easily on top of what you've already built — or steal across rows.

Direction A

Quiet chip

Lowest risk · lives inside existing UI · zero takeover

A single pill at the bottom of any screen. It's just a button when idle, breathes when listening, spins when thinking, fills with brand colour when speaking. Your app stays fully visible behind it the whole time.

Best for
  • Adding voice as a peer to text input
  • Apps where the existing screen is the value
  • Productivity / utility products

04 · Recommendation

If forced to pick one.

For JBIQ retro-fitting voice into the existing assistant, the safe-but-sharp pick is Direction F — Bottom-sheet halo.

It gives you a single, brandable purple orb (so voice has a face) without the cost of a fullscreen takeover. The user's existing screen — search results, JioMart, recharge flows — stays in view, which preserves all the work you've already done in the rest of the product.

If voice becomes a destination — a coach, a personal helper — push to Direction B. If voice should disappear into the chrome — just another way to type a query — push to Direction A.

Next, in Figma

  1. Pick a direction (or two to bake-off internally).
  2. Lock the four states as a single component with variants — don't draw them as separate screens.
  3. Define the breath cycle (recommend 2.4s) and use it everywhere — listening, speaking, idle hover.
  4. Add live transcript regardless of direction. It's the single highest-trust feature.
  5. Prototype the transitions, not the states. That's where this design lives or dies.
